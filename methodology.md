# Methodology

How PlainSpeak measures reading difficulty, flags jargon, and drafts a plainer version. Everything below runs client-side in `index.html`. No text is sent anywhere.

## 1. Reading-level scoring

PlainSpeak uses two well-established readability measures. Both rely on three counts from the text: **sentences**, **words**, and **syllables**.

**Flesch–Kincaid Grade Level** — estimates the U.S. school grade needed to read the text on a first pass:

```
0.39 × (words ÷ sentences) + 11.8 × (syllables ÷ words) − 15.59
```

A result of 8.0 means roughly an eighth-grade reading level. Higher means harder.

**Flesch Reading Ease** — scores readability from 0 to 100, where higher is easier:

```
206.835 − 1.015 × (words ÷ sentences) − 84.6 × (syllables ÷ words)
```

Roughly: 60–70 is plain and conversational; below 30 reads as dense and academic.

Both formulas reward two things — shorter sentences and shorter words. That is exactly the behavior plain-language writing aims for, which is why they're a reasonable proxy here.

**Counting details.** Sentences are split on `.`, `!`, and `?`. Words are alphanumeric tokens. Syllables are estimated with a vowel-group heuristic (counting runs of vowels, trimming common silent endings, with a floor of one syllable per word). The heuristic is fast and license-free, but it is an estimate — see Limitations.

## 2. Jargon flagging

The tool checks the text against a curated dictionary of common bureaucratic terms and phrases, each paired with an everyday replacement (for example, *utilize → use*, *pursuant to → under*, *in the event that → if*). Multi-word phrases are matched before single words so the longest, most specific match wins. Every flagged term is shown with its plain swap.

## 3. Plain-language rewrite

The rewrite is **rule-based**, not generated by a language model. It runs the text through a short pipeline:

1. Apply every plain-language swap from the jargon dictionary.
2. Turn semicolons into full stops, so each sentence carries one idea.
3. Break very long sentences (28+ words) at a coordinating conjunction near the middle.
4. Tidy spacing, punctuation, and capitalization.

Because the logic is transparent and runs in the browser, nothing about the source text is uploaded — which matters for sensitive drafts. The trade-off is that a rule-based pass can produce wording a human would phrase more gracefully, so the output is presented as a **first draft to review**, never a finished rewrite.

## 4. From grade level to stakes

A grade number on its own doesn't tell most readers much. PlainSpeak maps the original text's grade onto a plain-language statement about who it reaches:

| Reading grade | What it means |
|---|---|
| 6 or below | Most U.S. adults can read it comfortably — the target for public-facing writing. |
| 7–8 | Around the average U.S. adult reading level. |
| 9–11 | Above the level at which most adults read comfortably. |
| 12 and up | College reading level; excludes a large share of adults. |

This framing reflects a widely cited finding in adult-literacy research: a large share of U.S. adults read most comfortably at or below an eighth-grade level. The bands are a communication aid, not a precise population statistic.

## Limitations

- **Formulas measure form, not comprehension.** Flesch–Kincaid and Reading Ease count sentence and word length. They can't tell whether an idea is actually understandable, whether the logic flows, or whether a short sentence is still confusing.
- **Syllable counts are estimated.** The heuristic occasionally miscounts unusual or technical words, which nudges scores slightly.
- **English only.** The formulas and the jargon dictionary are built for English text.
- **The rewrite is a draft.** It can change tone, miss nuance, or phrase something awkwardly. A person should always confirm the meaning held before the rewrite is used.
- **Numbers are a starting point.** A good grade score is necessary but not sufficient for genuinely accessible writing. Plain words, clear structure, and testing with real readers — especially Disabled readers and people with cognitive disabilities — still matter most.
